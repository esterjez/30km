Why 30 km/h?

Slower streets, stronger communities.

In more and more cities, the maximum speed on local roads is being lowered to 30 km/h. Why? Because lower speeds make for safer streets, less noise and more space for people instead of cars.

What do real-world results show?

Several European cities have implemented the 30 km/h policy, and the results speak for themselves:

Brussels

saw a 25% reduction in serious traffic accidents since 2021.

Paris

reduced traffic noise by an average of 3 decibels.

Amsterdam

reports that 6 in 10 residents are positive about the introduction of 30 km/h.

Although lowering the speed to 30 km/h often has the same benefits, not every city has the same reason to implement this measure. Curious which city had which motivation? Check out the table below!

What are the challenges?

Changing speed means changing mindset.

Although the 30 km/h policy offers promising benefits, its implementation is not without obstacles. Here is an overview of the main challenges faced.

Effect not always easy to measure

Although local studies often show positive results, it is difficult to draw hard conclusions on a large scale. Many cities lack reliable data or good evaluation methods.

Behavior does not change by itself

A sign with “30” is not enough. Without enforcement or physical measures (such as speed bumps or narrowing), many drivers continue to display their old behavior.

Lack of uniformity

Each city approaches it differently. Some only use traffic signs, others combine it with street redesign. This makes it difficult to compare results.

Doubts among residents

If expectations are raised too high, but the effects are only visible to a limited extent, this can lead to disappointment or resistance. Transparent communication about what can and cannot be expected is essential.

How to successfully implement the 30 km/h policy?

The transition to a city with a 30 km/h standard requires a smart and well-coordinated approach:

Legal foundation

It starts with setting up a solid legal framework. Existing traffic rules must be adjusted and coordinated with local authorities, transport services and the police.

Public involvement

Creating support is crucial. Through clear campaigns with media, signs, flyers and neighbourhood meetings, citizens and politicians learn why this measure works. The better the explanation, the greater the support.

Stimulating active mobility

A safe city is a city where people walk, cycle and take public transport. Investing in wider cycle paths, more public transport and car-free zones helps people to choose alternatives.

Traffic calming measures

Speed limiters such as speed bumps, raised intersections and narrower lanes ensure that 30 km/h is not just a sign, but the new norm on the streets.

Smart technology

Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS) can help adjust traffic lights to traffic congestion. This makes the city safer and more efficient.

The following guidance documents can support governments in the design and implementation of traffic calming measures:

Low-Speed Zone Guide developed by the Global Road Safety Facility (World Bank) and the World Resources Institute;

Global Street Design Guide developed by the Global Designing Cities Initiative;

City Limits developed by the National Association of City Transportation Officials;

Road Safety Toolkit developed by the International Road Assessment Programme (iRAP);

Guide for Determining Readiness for Speed Cameras and Other Automated Enforcement developed by the Global Road Safety Facility and the Global Road Safety Partnership.
