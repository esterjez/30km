Why this city hit the brakes?

Helsinki's 30 km/h speed limit policy aims to improve road safety and support sustainable transport. It forms part of the city's goal to halve traffic deaths and injuries by 2030 and eliminate them entirely by 2050. By making streets safer, Helsinki also encourages walking, cycling, and public transport over private car use.

The aim was to:
1. Reduce traffic accidents and fatalities.
2. Promote sustainable transport

Key takeaways and lessons learned

Public consultation and tailored approaches by area type helped secure support. Infrastructure such as speed bumps and visual cues were used, particularly near schools and residential areas.

Countdown to 30: What It Took to Prepare

Phased implementation of speed limits: Starting in 2004, Helsinki introduced 30 km/h zones in residential areas and the city centre, expanding them in 2019 to encompass most streets.

Public consultations: Engagements were held with various stakeholders, including residents, businesses, and advocacy groups, to gather input and build consensus on the changes.

Infrastructure modifications: The city employed traffic calming measures such as speed bumps to naturally slow down traffic.

Strengthening traffic enforcement: Helsinki plans to install 70 automatic speed control systems to enforce the new speed limits. By the end of 2023, 33 of them have been installed.

From Numbers to Neighbourhoods: Implementation

Tailored approach by street type: Helsinki customized its speed reductions depending on the character of each area. In central neighborhoods like Kallio and Punavuori where walking and cycling dominate and street activity is high, limits were lowered to 30 km/h on most local roads. In more car-oriented or connector roads outside the center, such as sections of Mannerheimintie, limits remained at 40–50 km/h depending on traffic composition and infrastructure.

Focus on school zones: Speed limits were dropped to 30 km/h near more than 120 schools and kindergartens. Visual clues like color-coded were deployed to improve driver awareness in these areas.

Redesign of street geometry in more than 50 areas: These included raised crosswalks, narrower lanes in residential zones like Käpylä, and curb extensions to slow turning vehicles.

Integrated cycling and pedestrian upgrades help to encourage bike commuting while improving safety.




Slower, Safer, Better?




Safer Roads

Between 2016 and 2022, the number of pedestrian and cyclist deaths fell by approximately 75%, contributing to one of the lowest road fatality rates among European capitals. This significant drop shows the long-term effect of sustained speed reduction and prioritizing vulnerable road users.

Calmer Roads

Injuries to vulnerable road users fell by almost 30% in areas where lower speed limits were introduced, compared with baseline data from the early 2010s. Slower speeds mean fewer severe collisions and a safer environment for everyone.

Perceived Safety

Residents reported feeling significantly safer, particularly families and older citizens. Lower speeds near schools, parks, and shopping streets have made walking and cycling more appealing and accessible in daily life.

Smart Enforcement

Helsinki integrates speed enforcement with technology: 70 automatic speed control systems are planned, with 33 already in place by the end of 2023. These tools not only increase compliance but also provide valuable data to evaluate and adjust mobility policies over time.
