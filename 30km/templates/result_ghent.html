Why this city hit the brakes?

Ghent has introduced 30 km/h speed limits across the city to improve safety in the city, promote sustainable mobility and improve environmental quality.

The aim was to:
1. Improve road safety
2. Promote sustainable transport

Key takeaways and lessons learned

Aligning speed limit policies with broader mobility and climate strategies ensures cohesive urban development.

Investing in infrastructure. Additional infrastructure such as cycling facilities is critical to supporting behavioural shifts towards sustainable transport.

Data-driven approach. Monitoring and evaluating results has enabled informed adjustments and demonstrated the effectiveness of policies.

Countdown to 30: What It Took to Prepare

Holistic planning: The 30 km/h zones were part of the wider Ghent Sustainable Urban Mobility Plan (SUMP), which aims to switch 50% of transport modes to sustainable ones by 2030.

Infrastructure upgrades: Clear signs and road markings were installed to indicate the new speed zones.

Public awareness campaigns: Initiatives such as "Gent daagt uit" ("Ghent Challenges") were launched to inform residents about the new speed limits and their benefits.

Enforcement measures: Initial stages focused on raising awareness, followed by stricter enforcement with fines for non-compliance.

From Numbers to Neighbourhoods: Implementation

Stage-by-stage implementation. Since 2015, 30 km/h zones have been gradually introduced in various neighbourhoods, including the Mariakerke and Vondelgem.

Comprehensive coverage. By 2018, most of Ghent's city centre and surrounding areas were covered by 30 km/h zones.

Additional measures. Developing extensive cycling infrastructure, including over 500 kilometres of cycle paths and over 60 cycle bridges and underpasses, to support the transition to sustainable transport.




Slower, Safer, Better?




Reduced road accidents

One year after implementation, the number of road accidents had decreased by 10%, and the number of serious injuries had decreased by 26%

Lower speed, Big Impact

On 29 monitored streets, the 85th percentile speed dropped from 40 km/h to 36 km/h.

Mobility pattern change

Cycling increased from 22% to 37%, and public transport use increased by 6%.

Air quality improvement

The Low Emission Zone introduced in 2020 resulted in a 52% reduction in soot particles in the city centre.
