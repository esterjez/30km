Why this city hit the brakes?

The city's key concerns are road safety, air pollution, and the quality of the urban environment. According to research, reducing the speed limit from 50 km/h to 30 km/h significantly lowers the risk of fatalities in collisions with pedestrians

The aim was to:
1. Improve road safety
2. Decrease pollution
3. Improve livability

Key takeaways and lessons learned

Piloting First Built Confidence and Legitimacy. Madrid's 2016 pilot in the Chamberí district allowed the city to demonstrate real benefits—like improved safety and reduced pollution—before expanding citywide. This step-by-step approach gave policymakers evidence to justify broader changes and helped build public trust.

National Policy Backing Enabled Long-Term Change. Madrid's early local actions laid the groundwork for Spain's national law in 2021 mandating 30 km/h on urban single-lane roads. This alignment between city and state created lasting impact, ensured consistency across regions, and gave cities stronger tools for enforcement.

Framing Speed Reduction as Public Health, Not Just Transport. By emphasizing the protection of vulnerable groups—like older pedestrians and children—and linking speed reduction to cleaner air and quieter streets, Madrid positioned the policy as a quality-of-life issue. This broadened its appeal and helped overcome resistance to change

From Numbers to Neighbourhoods: Implementation

Madrid began implementing 30 km/h limits in September 2018, applying them to all streets with one lane in each direction—covering approximately 80% of the city's street network.

The effort gained national momentum in May 2021, when Spain introduced a countrywide law mandating 30 km/h limits on comparable urban roads, effectively scaling Madrid's local initiative across the nation.

The city introduced shared car-bike lanes with a 30 km/h speed cap, combined pedestrian-bike paths in mixed-use areas, and segregated cycle tracks for busier routes. This mix of infrastructure types allowed for tailored solutions based on neighborhood needs and traffic conditions.




Slower, Safer, Better?




Slower Speeds, Safer Streets

The introduction of the 30 km/h speed limit in Madrid aimed to improve road safety, particularly for vulnerable road users such as pedestrians and cyclists.

Promotion of Active Mobility

The 30 km/h speed limit has encouraged the use of active transportation modes. Madrid has expanded its cycling infrastructure, including shared car-bike lanes, combined pedestrian-bike paths, and segregated cycle tracks, making the city more accommodating for cyclists and pedestrians.

Environmental improvements

Lower speed limits contribute to environmental benefits. On average, cities have observed an 18% decrease in emissions, a 2.5 dB reduction in noise pollution, and a 7% drop in fuel consumption following the implementation of 30 km/h zones.
