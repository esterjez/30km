Why this city hit the brakes?

Oslo’s adoption of reduced speed limits is rooted in its commitment to the Vision Zero initiative, aiming to eliminate all traffic fatalities and severe injuries. Recognizing the disproportionate risk faced by pedestrians and cyclists, the city prioritized lowering speed limits to enhance safety for these vulnerable groups.

The aim was to:
1. Reduce traffic-related fatalities and injuries
2. Enhance urban livability

Key takeaways and lessons learned

Holistic strategy. Combining infrastructure changes, policy enforcement, and public engagement creates a comprehensive safety net. The city installed approximately 500 speed humps and introduced curb extensions to naturally slow down traffic.

Community involvement. Engaging residents in planning and monitoring fosters a sense of ownership and compliance.

Data-driven decisions. Continuous monitoring and analysis ensure that policies are effective and adjusted as needed.

Countdown to 30: What It Took to Prepare

Educational campaigns:The city launched initiatives to educate drivers, cyclists, and pedestrians about road safety and the benefits of reduced speed limits.

Community speed watch programs Residents participated in monitoring traffic speeds, fostering a collective responsibility for road safety.

School programs: Children were educated on traffic safety, and schools were involved in planning and implementing safety measures.

From Numbers to Neighbourhoods: Implementation

Differentiated speed zones: Residential areas were designated with 30 km/h limits, streets with bus traffic at 40 km/h, and main arterial roads at 50 km/h.

Reallocation of road space: Oslo removed over 1,000 street-side parking spots, replacing them with bike lanes and pedestrian areas to prioritize non-motorized transport.

School zone safety initiatives: "Heart zones" were established around schools, restricting car access during peak hours to protect children. The city aimed to create 100 such zones over four years.




Slower, Safer, Better?




Zero pedestrian and cyclist fatalities

In 2019, Oslo recorded no deaths among pedestrians and cyclists, a historic achievement for the city.

Increased active transportation

The safer environment led to a rise in walking and cycling, contributing to public health and environmental benefits.

Reduction in severe injuries

Between 2015 and 2020, the number of severe injuries decreased by nearly 40%, demonstrating the effectiveness of the implemented measures.
